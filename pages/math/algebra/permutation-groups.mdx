## Why This Topic Matters

The symmetric group $S_n$ (all permutations of $n$ elements) is the canonical group. Cayley's theorem says every group is a subgroup of $S_n$. In CP: permutation composition, counting under symmetry (Burnside), orbit problems, and functional graph analysis all use permutation group theory.

**Contest relevance**: CF Div.1 C/D/E, Burnside/Pólya counting, permutation puzzles, cycle-based problems.

---

## Symmetric Group $S_n$

The **symmetric group** $S_n$ consists of all bijections $\sigma: \{1, \ldots, n\} \to \{1, \ldots, n\}$ under composition.

- $|S_n| = n!$
- $S_n$ is non-abelian for $n \geq 3$
- Every element of $S_n$ can be written as a product of disjoint cycles

**Notation**: $\sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ means $\sigma(1)=2$, $\sigma(2)=3$, $\sigma(3)=1$ — this is the 3-cycle $(1\ 2\ 3)$.

```cpp
// Permutation as array: perm[i] = sigma(i)
using Perm = vector<int>;

Perm compose(const Perm& a, const Perm& b) {
    // (a ∘ b)(i) = a(b(i))
    int n = a.size();
    Perm c(n);
    for (int i = 0; i < n; i++) c[i] = a[b[i]];
    return c;
}

Perm inverse(const Perm& p) {
    int n = p.size();
    Perm inv(n);
    for (int i = 0; i < n; i++) inv[p[i]] = i;
    return inv;
}
```

## Key Subgroups of $S_n$

### Alternating Group $A_n$

$A_n = $ even permutations in $S_n$ (permutations expressible as product of even number of transpositions).

- $|A_n| = n!/2$
- $A_n \trianglelefteq S_n$ (normal subgroup)
- $A_n$ is simple (no proper normal subgroups) for $n \geq 5$ — one of the most important results in algebra

### Cyclic Group $C_n \subseteq S_n$

Generated by the single $n$-cycle $(1\ 2\ 3\ \cdots\ n)$. Order $n$.

### Dihedral Group $D_n \subseteq S_{2n}$

Symmetries of regular $n$-gon: $n$ rotations + $n$ reflections. Order $2n$.

In Burnside/Pólya, $D_n$ acts on necklace positions.

## Cayley's Theorem

**Theorem**: Every group $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_{|G|}$.

**Proof**: Define the **left regular representation**: for each $g \in G$, define $\lambda_g: G \to G$ by $\lambda_g(h) = gh$. This $\lambda_g$ is a bijection (since $G$ is a group). The map $\phi: G \to S_{|G|}$, $g \mapsto \lambda_g$, is a homomorphism: $\phi(g_1 g_2)(h) = g_1 g_2 h = \lambda_{g_1}(\lambda_{g_2}(h)) = (\phi(g_1) \circ \phi(g_2))(h)$. It's injective: $\ker(\phi) = \{e\}$ since $\lambda_g = \lambda_e$ implies $g = e$. $\square$

**CP consequence**: any group structure can be represented as permutations and computed algorithmically.

## Group Actions and Orbits

$G$ **acts on** $X$ via $\alpha: G \times X \to X$ satisfying $e \cdot x = x$ and $(gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (hx)$.

**Orbit** of $x$: $\text{Orb}(x) = \{g \cdot x : g \in G\} \subseteq X$.

**Stabilizer** of $x$: $\text{Stab}(x) = \{g \in G : g \cdot x = x\} \leq G$.

**Orbit-Stabilizer Theorem**: $|G| = |\text{Orb}(x)| \cdot |\text{Stab}(x)|$.

The orbits partition $X$ (equivalence classes under "$x \sim y$ if $\exists g: g \cdot x = y$").

### Burnside's Lemma (Cauchy-Frobenius)

**Number of orbits** $= \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |X^g|$ where $X^g = \{x \in X : g \cdot x = x\}$.

**Proof**: Count pairs $(g, x)$ with $g \cdot x = x$ in two ways.
- Summing over $g$: $\sum_g |X^g|$
- Summing over $x$: $\sum_x |\text{Stab}(x)| = \sum_{\text{orbits}} \sum_{x \in O} |\text{Stab}(x)| = \sum_{\text{orbits}} |G|$ (by orbit-stabilizer, $\sum_{x \in O}|\text{Stab}(x)| = |O| \cdot |G|/|O| = |G|$).

Equating: $\sum_g |X^g| = (\text{num orbits}) \cdot |G|$, giving Burnside. $\square$

### Example: Necklace Counting

$n$-bead necklace with $k$ colors, up to rotation ($G = C_n$, $X = $ all colorings):

$|X^r|$ for rotation by $d$: colorings fixed by rotation by $d$ = colorings with period $d' = \gcd(d, n)$... actually $|X^r|$ for rotation by $d$ = $k^{\gcd(d,n)}$.

$$\text{necklaces} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d=0}^{n-1} k^{\gcd(d,n)} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d|n} \phi(d) \cdot k^{n/d}$$

## Transitive and Regular Actions

**Transitive**: $G$ acts transitively on $X$ if there is only one orbit (any $x, y \in X$ have $g \cdot x = y$ for some $g$).

**Regular (free and transitive)**: transitive and $|\text{Stab}(x)| = 1$ for all $x$. Then $|G| = |X|$.

**The left regular action** of $G$ on itself (used in Cayley's proof) is a regular action.

---

## Conjugacy Classes

Two elements $a, b \in G$ are **conjugate** if $\exists g: b = gag^{-1}$.

Conjugacy classes partition $G$. For $S_n$: two permutations are conjugate iff they have the same cycle type (same partition of $n$ as cycle lengths).

**Example** in $S_4$: cycle types $1^4, 2 \cdot 1^2, 2^2, 3 \cdot 1, 4$ — 5 conjugacy classes.

Number of conjugacy classes in $S_n$ = number of partitions of $n$.

**Class equation**: $|G| = \sum_C |C|$ where $C$ ranges over conjugacy classes, and $|C| = |G|/|C_G(g)|$ for any $g \in C$ (centralizer).
