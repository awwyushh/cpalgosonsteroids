## Why This Topic Matters

Algebraic structures unify seemingly different CP tools. Modular arithmetic = integers mod $p$ form a field. XOR = $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ vector space. NTT = Fourier transform over a cyclic group. Burnside's lemma = group actions. Understanding the structure tells you what operations are valid and why algorithms work.

**Contest relevance**: CF Div.1 D/E, problems involving symmetry (Burnside/Pólya), polynomial operations (field structure), combinatorics under group action.

---

## Groups

A **group** $(G, \cdot)$ is a set with a binary operation satisfying:
1. **Closure**: $a, b \in G \Rightarrow a \cdot b \in G$
2. **Associativity**: $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$
3. **Identity**: $\exists e \in G$ s.t. $e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$
4. **Inverse**: $\forall a \in G$, $\exists a^{-1}$ s.t. $a \cdot a^{-1} = e$

A group is **abelian (commutative)** if additionally $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$.

### Key Examples

| Group | Operation | Identity | Abelian? |
|-------|-----------|----------|---------|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | $+$ | $0$ | Yes |
| $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ | $+$ mod $n$ | $0$ | Yes |
| $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ | $\times$ mod $p$ | $1$ | Yes |
| $\text{GL}(n, F)$ | matrix multiply | $I_n$ | No ($n \geq 2$) |
| $S_n$ (permutations) | composition | identity perm | No ($n \geq 3$) |
| $C_n$ (cyclic group) | $+$ mod $n$ | $0$ | Yes |
| $D_n$ (dihedral) | rotation+reflection | identity | No |

## Subgroups and Lagrange's Theorem

$H \subseteq G$ is a **subgroup** if $H$ is a group under the same operation.

**Lagrange's Theorem**: If $G$ is finite and $H \leq G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$.

**Proof**: Define cosets $aH = \{ah : h \in H\}$ for $a \in G$. Two cosets are either equal or disjoint (proof: if $aH \cap bH \neq \emptyset$, then $a^{-1}b \in H$, so $bH = aH$). Each coset has size $|H|$. The cosets partition $G$, so $|G| = [G:H] \cdot |H|$ where $[G:H]$ is the number of cosets. $\square$

**Corollary**: Order of any element $g$ divides $|G|$. So $g^{|G|} = e$ — this is exactly Euler's theorem ($a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1$) and Fermat's little theorem ($a^{p-1} \equiv 1$) in disguise.

**Order of an element**: $\text{ord}(g) = $ smallest $k > 0$ with $g^k = e$.

---

## Cyclic Groups

$G$ is **cyclic** if $G = \langle g \rangle = \{g^0, g^1, g^2, \ldots\}$ for some generator $g$.

**Theorem**: Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.

**Theorem**: $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ has exactly one subgroup of order $d$ for each $d | n$.

**Theorem**: $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ is cyclic of order $p-1$. A generator is a **primitive root** mod $p$.

## Group Homomorphisms

A **homomorphism** $\phi: G \to H$ satisfies $\phi(ab) = \phi(a)\phi(b)$.

- **Kernel**: $\ker(\phi) = \{g \in G : \phi(g) = e_H\}$ — always a normal subgroup
- **Image**: $\text{im}(\phi) \subseteq H$ — a subgroup of $H$
- **Isomorphism**: $\phi$ is bijective; then $G \cong H$ (structurally identical)

**First Isomorphism Theorem**: $G / \ker(\phi) \cong \text{im}(\phi)$.

**CP relevance**: the map $a \mapsto a \bmod n$ is a ring homomorphism $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$. The map $k \mapsto g^k$ is an isomorphism $\mathbb{Z}/(p-1)\mathbb{Z} \to (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ (when $g$ is a primitive root).

## Normal Subgroups and Quotient Groups

$N \leq G$ is **normal** ($N \trianglelefteq G$) if $gNg^{-1} = N$ for all $g \in G$.

Equivalently: left cosets = right cosets.

The **quotient group** $G/N = \{gN : g \in G\}$ with operation $(aN)(bN) = (ab)N$.

**Example**: $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z} / n\mathbb{Z}$ — integers modulo $n$ as quotient of integers by the subgroup $n\mathbb{Z}$.

## Rings

A **ring** $(R, +, \cdot)$ satisfies:
- $(R, +)$ is an abelian group
- $(R, \cdot)$ is associative with identity $1$
- Distributive: $a(b+c) = ab + ac$ and $(b+c)a = ba + ca$

A ring is **commutative** if $ab = ba$.

### Examples

| Ring | Notes |
|------|-------|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | No inverses for multiplication |
| $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ | Integers mod $n$ |
| $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ | Polynomials with integer coefficients |
| $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ | Polynomials mod prime $p$ |
| $M_n(F)$ | $n \times n$ matrices over field $F$ |

**Ideal**: $I \subseteq R$ with $RI \subseteq I$ and $IR \subseteq I$ — the "kernel" analogue for rings. Quotient ring $R/I$ is defined similarly.

**Zero divisors**: $a \neq 0$ and $b \neq 0$ with $ab = 0$ — these prevent inverses. $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$: $2 \cdot 3 = 0$.

An **integral domain** is a commutative ring with no zero divisors.

---

## Fields

A **field** $(F, +, \cdot)$ is a commutative ring where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse.

Equivalently: $(F, +)$ is abelian group, $(F^*, \cdot)$ is abelian group, and distributivity holds.

### Examples

| Field | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| $\mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}$ | Infinite fields |
| $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ | $p$ prime; finite field of order $p$ |
| $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$ | Finite field of order $p^k$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ | Extension of rationals |

**Characteristic**: smallest $n > 0$ with $n \cdot 1 = 0$ (or $\infty$). For $\mathbb{F}_p$: characteristic $p$. For $\mathbb{R}$: characteristic 0.

**Theorem**: Every finite field has order $p^k$ for some prime $p$ and $k \geq 1$. For each $p^k$, there is exactly one finite field $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$ (up to isomorphism).

| Finite fields | $\vert \mathbb{F} \vert = p^k$, unique | NTT, GF(2) |
| Orbit-Stabilizer | $\vert G \vert = \vert Orb \vert \cdot \vert Stab \vert$ | Burnside's lemma |

## Group Actions

$G$ **acts on** set $X$ if there is a map $G \times X \to X$, $(g, x) \mapsto g \cdot x$, with:
- $e \cdot x = x$
- $(gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)$

**Orbit** of $x$: $Orb(x) = \{g \cdot x : g \in G\}$.

**Stabilizer** of $x$: $Stab(x) = \{g \in G : g \cdot x = x\}$ — a subgroup of $G$.

**Orbit-Stabilizer Theorem**: $|G| = |Orb(x)| \cdot |Stab(x)|$.

**Burnside's Lemma**: number of orbits $= \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |X^g|$ where $X^g = \{x : g \cdot x = x\}$ (fixed points).

**Proof of Orbit-Stabilizer**: The map $G/Stab(x) \to Orb(x)$, $gStab(x) \mapsto g \cdot x$, is a bijection (well-defined, injective, surjective). So $|Orb(x)| = [G : Stab(x)] = |G|/|Stab(x)|$. $\square$
